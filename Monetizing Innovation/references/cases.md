# Monetizing Innovation — Case Studies

Pattern-matching reference for diagnosing and planning launches.

---

## Failure Cases

### Amazon Fire Phone — Feature Shock
- $170M write-down; price cut $199 → $0.99 post-launch
- Features crammed in (Dynamic Perspective, Firefly) without clear customer value
- No one could articulate why a customer would choose it

### Audi Q7 — Minivation
- Launched at €55K; optimal price was €58K
- Missed €210M/year from pricing too safely below WTP

### Silicon Valley Component Maker — Minivation
- Charged 85¢ when customer captured $5 of value
- Left a 6x value gap on the table for the customer

### Kodak Digital Camera — Hidden Gem
- Invented digital photography in 1974; didn't ship until 1995
- Mid-level execs killed it to protect the film business

### Segway — Undead
- $100M development; sold ~30K total over 6 years
- Wrong market; no validated customer need

### Google Glass — Undead
- Wrong segment (consumers vs surgeons/industrial users)
- Top-down pet project; no customer dissent permitted

### Dodge Dart 2012 — Anti-Cayenne (Textbook Failure)
- Fiat Chrysler bragged in TV ads they "kicked the finance guys out"
- Designed for engineering perfection, then priced
- 2012: 25,000 sold (1/4 of forecast); 2nd biggest new-product flop of 2012
- 2015: Sold 1/7th of Corolla + Civic combined

### LivingSocial — Penetration Disaster
- Raised $900M; lost $1B in 4 years
- *NYT*: "No one paid much attention to how the company would ultimately make money"

### European Market Leader — Price Integrity Collapse
- 70% market share in Holland
- Competitor attacked; sales head panicked and asked CEO for discount approval
- Pricing strategy collapsed in a single day

## Success Cases

### Porsche Cayenne & Panamera — The Gold Standard
- Customer research 4 years before launch
- Car clinics with WTP questions (most automakers skip WTP)
- "Burden of proof is always on why a feature should be INCLUDED"
- Living business case modeled cannibalization
- Skimming strategy: 8-cylinder launched first, 6-cylinder a year later
- C-suite drove the process personally
- Result: ~50% of Porsche's profit now comes from Cayenne
**Lesson:** WTP-first + C-suite ownership + skimming + cannibalization modeling = gold standard.

### Gillette Guard (India) — Design Around the Price
- Indian market potential: 4x US (400M men); Mach 3 at 100 rupees was too expensive
- Spent thousands of hours interviewing Indians BEFORE design
- Target price: 15 rupees razor, 5 rupees blade
- Re-engineered product to hit that price: cut 25 components → 4
- Result: >60% Indian market share within 2 years (3x previous)
**Lesson:** Design product around the price — literally. Rebuild the cost structure.

### LinkedIn — Marketplace Pricing Evolution
- "Members first" principle drives all decisions
- InMail evolved as marketplace grew: started as pay-only-on-response → shifted as network scaled
- Monetizes BOTH sides of the marketplace
- Talent Solutions = $1.3B business
- Process: hypothesis → internal refinement → customer validation → sales gut-check → quantitative model → **paid pilots (not free betas)**
**Lesson:** Paid pilots > free betas. Monetization models must evolve with marketplace scale.

### Dräger Safety — Field Research Over Guessing
- Replaced "engineer brainstorming + sales anecdotes" with field research
- CEO went to sewers and pipelines to interview workers personally
- 5-phase Customer Process Monitoring: define target → identify decision makers → qualitative research → quantitative WTP → assess competitors
- X-zone 5000: 35% premium, 250% over forecast
**Lesson:** CEOs go to the field. Field research replaces guessing.

### Uber — Dynamic Pricing Solves Supply
- Both rider and driver elasticity are "off the charts"
- Surge pricing solves the supply problem (originally called "availability pricing")
- Penetration pricing tied to virtuous cycle: more usage → lower cost → lower price → more demand
- No tipping by design — removes anxiety and decision burden
**Lesson:** Dynamic pricing can solve supply problems, not just demand. Penetration only works tied to a real network effect.

### Swarovski — Research Drivers, Not SKUs
- 100,000+ SKUs — can't research each one
- Researched drivers of value instead (cut complexity + brilliance)
- 5 tiers; price span of 650% from low to high
- Phone case crystals: 120% premium accepted
**Lesson:** When SKU count is huge, research what drives value — not individual SKUs.

### Optimizely — Choose the Right Metric
- Started WTP conversations BEFORE writing code
- Cross-functional monetization task force
- Chose Monthly Unique Visitors (MUVs) over per-user (per-user would have been a Minivation)
- Personalization product: chose total site traffic as metric (different from Testing's MUVs)
- Validated that customers preferred Testing and Personalization sold separately, not bundled
**Lesson:** The wrong metric is a Minivation. Always validate your bundling assumptions.

### Apple Watch — Price Integrity Under Pressure
- Launched April 2015 at $349
- WSJ headline: "Glimmers Emerge on Apple Watch Sales, and They're Not Pretty"
- Apple did NOT drop the price
- Result: ~8M sold in first 6 months = $2.8B
**Lesson:** Holding price through bad press validates premium positioning.

### BMW 7 Series (2001) — Diagnose Before Cutting
- Slow sales. Root cause: Europeans thought the design was ugly
- BMW did NOT cut the price
- Reduced sales targets; started a face-lift redesign
- Recovery followed the redesign
**Lesson:** Slow sales is rarely a pricing problem. Diagnose first.

### Mercedes SL — Underpricing Is Also a Problem
- Sales blew through forecast; 2-year waitlist
- Internal admission: 20% underpriced
- "Left hundreds of millions on the table"
**Lesson:** Sellouts and waitlists = underpriced. High sales can be just as bad as low sales.

### Michelin — The Alternative Metric
- New tire lasted 20% longer but could only command a few % premium per unit
- Problem: longer life means customers buy 20% LESS often
- Solution: charge by miles driven, not per tire
- Result: 20% longer life = 20% MORE revenue; 25% higher EBIT than Bridgestone, 3x Goodyear
**Lesson:** How you charge can be more valuable than what you charge.
