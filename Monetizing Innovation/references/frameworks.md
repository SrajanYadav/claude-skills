## Rule 1: Have the WTP Talk Early

WTP = what customers value AND how much they'll pay for it.

### The 5 Question Methods (easy → advanced)
1. **Direct** — "What's acceptable? Expensive? Prohibitively expensive?"
2. **Purchase probability** — "Would you buy this for $X?"
3. **Most-least analysis** — "Which feature matters most? Least?"
4. **Build-your-own** — Customer assembles their ideal package
5. **Purchase simulations (conjoint)** — Show options at different prices; requires 200+ respondents

### 10 Insights for WTP Conversations
1. Tap internal expertise first — cross-functional pre-meeting
2. Frame as "value talks," not "pricing talks" — switch to price after rapport
3. Start with simple direct questions
4. **25% of questions should be "Why?"**
5. Mix structured + unstructured
6. Get the whole cross-functional team in the room
7. Avoid the "average trap" — look at distribution, not mean
8. Don't rely only on quantitative data
9. Be precise: "Would you buy this?" ≠ "Would you buy this for $20?"
10. Garbage in, garbage out — keep questions simple

## Rule 2: Needs-Based Segmentation

> "Demographic segmentation is broken."

### The Prince Charles vs Ozzy Test
A 67-year-old wealthy Englishman with two kids and a castle = both Prince Charles AND Ozzy Osbourne. Same demographics, completely different needs.

### The Only Right Way to Segment for Innovation
By **needs, value, and willingness to pay** — not demographics, not behavior.

### Paper Company Case (4 Segments)
| Segment | Wants | WTP |
|---|---|---|
| Price only | Basic quality | Lowest |
| Want it now | Speedy delivery | Premium for speed |
| Product only | Performance & quality | Premium for product |
| Want the best | Quality + service + reliability | Highest |
Each segment had a mix of customer sizes. Company size meant nothing.

### 5 Segmentation Best Practices
1. Begin with WTP data
2. Common sense > statistics — acid test: can sales sort customers into segments without a spreadsheet?
3. Fewer is more — start with 3–4
4. Don't try to serve every segment
5. Describe segments observably so marketing can target them

### 3 Segmentation Pitfalls
1. Segmenting too late (only for marketing, not product design)
2. Segmenting only by observable characteristics
3. Too many schemes — if more than 3, you're confused

## Rule 3: Configuration & Bundling

### Leaders / Fillers / Killers
| Type | Definition | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Leader | Drives buying, high WTP | Always include |
| Filler | Nice-to-have | Use to fill gaps |
| Killer | Blows the deal if forced to pay | Remove or sell à la carte |

**Killer test:** Valued by <20% AND not valued at all by >20%. Always segment-specific.

### Good / Better / Best
Three-tier offering. People avoid extremes; the middle wins.

**Healthy distribution:** ≤25% Good / ~70% Better+Best / ≥10% Best
**Trip-wire:** >50% picking Good → strip features from Good tier

### Fences (What Makes G/B/B Work)
- Can customer see in 10 seconds what's missing from Good?
- Do they feel they "lost something" buying Good vs Better?
- Are differences emotional (status, support) or just cosmetic?

### Three Bundling Strategies
| Strategy | When to Use |
|---|---|
| Mixed bundle (bundle + à la carte) | DEFAULT for most situations |
| Pure bundle (only the bundle) | Rarely |
| Hard bundle (no à la carte) | Only with market dominance |
| Unbundle | Highly price-sensitive component (Ryanair: 2x more sensitive to base fare than fees) |

### 10 Bundling Insights
1. Align each bundle with a specific segment
2. Max 9 benefits or 4 products before psychological overload
3. Both sides must benefit — you AND customer
4. Don't give too much in entry-level or you can't upsell
5. Hard bundling rarely works — use mixed
6. In mixed bundles, à la carte prices must be HIGHER than pure à la carte (pizza $8 standalone → $9 when bundle exists)
7. Communicate creatively — "40% off Product A" beats "20% off bundle" (same math)
8. Sometimes 1+1=3 — charge premium for integrated bundles
9. Don't bundle for the sake of bundling — you're discounting buyers who'd pay full price
10. Exploit inverse correlations — bundle when segment 1 wants A and segment 2 wants B

## Rule 4: How You Charge Trumps What You Charge

### The Michelin Case
New tire lasted 20% longer. But longer life = customers buy 20% LESS often.
**Solution:** Charge by miles driven, not per tire.
Result: 20% longer life = 20% MORE revenue. 25% higher EBIT than Bridgestone, 3x Goodyear.

### The 5 Questions to Choose a Model
1. Will customers accept it?
2. How will future trends impact it?
3. Does it fit your company's stage? (Startups: simple. Mature: differentiated)
4. What are competitors doing? (differentiate, don't mimic)
5. How hard is it to implement?

### Price Structures Within a Model
- **Flat:** Same price for all
- **Volume tiers (1-D):** More usage = better rate
- **Matrix (2-D):** Two dimensions (e.g., volume × share-of-wallet)

## Rule 5: Pricing Strategy

### The 3 Pricing Strategy Types
| Strategy | When | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Maximization** | No early adopters with high WTP; little gap between short/long-term price | Most products |
| **Penetration** | Network effects; first-mover advantage; can hike later | Samsung, Uber, Facebook |
| **Skimming** | Big WTP gap between early/late adopters; bragging-rights segment | iPhone, HDTVs, Porsche Panamera |

**Penetration warning:** Riskiest from profit standpoint. LivingSocial: raised $900M, lost $1B in 4 years.

### Price-Setting Principles (Define Before Launch)
1. Which monetization model? Same across segments?
2. Price differentiation by channel, region, vertical?
3. Price floors — hard floor, max discount?
4. Price endings — B2C: .99, .95, .50; **B2B: whole numbers**
5. When and how much to increase?

### War-Game Before Launch (Reaction Principles)
- How will competitors react?
- One-time or sustained response?
- If we match price, what's the P&L impact?
- What's our counter-reaction?

## Rule 6: Living Business Case

### The 4 Pillars (Always Linked)
1. **Price** (from WTP)
2. **Value** (to customer)
3. **Volume** (demand at each price point)
4. **Cost** (variable + fixed + risk)

Change one → all others ripple. Model the linkages explicitly.

### 9 Steps to Build a Living Business Case
1. Forget your old static funding-document business cases
2. Assemble: market size, segments, configuration, value, WTP, model, costs, competitors
3. Include price elasticity (most companies skip this)
4. Use data-verified facts — don't overstate market size or adoption speed
5. Add risk assumptions — best/worst case; consider Monte Carlo
6. Be realistic about goal trade-offs — can't maximize all goals simultaneously
7. Quantify competitive reactions
8. Model cannibalization of existing products
9. Keep updating at every milestone

## Rule 7: Value Communication

> "A feature belongs to the product; a benefit belongs to the customer." — Drucker

**Reality check:** Salesperson has 10 minutes. Customer stays on website 5 minutes. Ad runs 15 seconds.

### The 3 Steps
1. **Develop crystal-clear benefit statements** (not features)
   - For each feature ask: "What does the customer ACHIEVE?"
   - Edison didn't sell "tungsten filament tested 1,000 times" — he sold "no more tripping over toys in the dark"
2. **Make statements segment-specific**
   - Adobe Individuals: "all the creative apps + 45M stock images"
   - Adobe Enterprise: "customized provisioning, license management"
3. **Measure impact, refine continuously** — run MOCA regularly

### MOCA Matrix
```
                    HIGH IMPORTANCE
                          |
   COMPETITIVE            |    EMPHASIZE
   DISADVANTAGE           |    THESE
   (Defend here)          |    (Lead here)
                          |
   ─────────────────────────────────────── Performance vs Competitor
                          |
   IGNORE FOR NOW         |    TRY TO
                          |    CONVINCE (only with proof)
                          |
                    LOW IMPORTANCE
```
⚠️ **Critical caveat:** Performance is as YOUR CUSTOMERS see it — not how you see it. Engineers fail MOCA by self-grading.

### Why Value Messaging Fails
Root cause: Sales/marketing brought in too late. They weren't part of the value story when the product was designed.
Fix: Add marketing/sales to the innovation team from day 1.

## Rule 8: Behavioral Pricing

### The 6 Tactics

**1. Compromise Effect**
Wine store: $10 / $25 / $40. Most pick $25. Always have a compromise option.

**2. Anchoring**
The Economist test: Adding $125 print-only made $125 print+online pick rate jump from 32% → 84%.
Always start B2B negotiations with a HIGH anchor.

**3. Price Signals Quality**
Ariely 2008: Same placebo at $2.50/pill → 85% pain relief. At $0.10/pill → 61% pain relief.
iPhone launched at $599 — at $49, Apple would have killed smartphone profitability forever.

**4. Razor / Razor Blades**
Coffee machine A: $480 machine + $10/mo coffee. Machine B: $120 + $40/mo coffee. Same 12-month total.
Customers strongly prefer B. Use ONLY if you're 100% sure of downstream sales.

**5. Pennies-a-Day**
Amazon EC2: prices in fractions of a cent per hour. $9.99/mo converts very differently from $120/yr.

**6. Psychological Thresholds**
$71 didn't sell. $69.99 did. That $1.01 difference dropped acceptance >20%.
Common thresholds: $40, $70, $99, $100, $199. Find yours. Stay on the cliff.

## Rule 9: Maintain Price Integrity

### The 4 Varieties of Post-Launch Problems
1. Market doesn't understand the value
2. Competition undercuts on price
3. Competition launches a competing product
4. Sales below plan regardless

Don't assume #4 is a price problem. Could be: feature gap, distribution, awareness, quality, sales training.

### The Three Non-Price Actions Rule
Before any price cut, exhaust these first:
1. Increase advertising
2. Add product value
3. Upgrade customer to higher-end at same price

### Healthy Escalation Rates
- 20–30% of deals should escalate (industry-dependent)
- 30–40% of ALL deals should have price changes upon escalation
- If approved prices = requested prices → rubber-stamping (bad)
- Too few escalations → you're underpriced

### On Price Wars
- 83% of companies feel price pressure; 58% say they're in a price war
- Of those: 90% say competitors started it (statistically impossible)
- It takes two to tango. Don't move first. Don't react from fear.
- Price wars have one winner: the supplier with the lowest cost. Is that you?
