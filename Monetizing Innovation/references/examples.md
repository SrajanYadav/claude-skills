## Pizza & Breadsticks Bundling Math

4 segments, 100 customers each, different WTP.

| Strategy | Revenue |
|---|---|
| Pizza $4.50 + Breadsticks $5 (à la carte) | $2,850 |
| Pizza $8 + Breadsticks $8.50 (optimal à la carte) | $3,300 |
| Bundle only at $10.50 (pure bundle) | $4,200 |
| **Mixed: Bundle $13 + Pizza $9 + Breadsticks $9** | **$4,400 (+33%)** |

**Less than 10% of executives find the optimal answer.**

Key insight: In mixed bundles, the à la carte prices must be HIGHER than they were in pure à la carte. This is counterintuitive. The bundle at $13 looks like a $5 discount vs $18 standalone — you show discounting without discounting.

## The 100-Point Goal Allocation Exercise

Have each C-suite exec independently allocate 100 points across goals.

| Goal | Exec A | Exec B | Exec C | CEO | Avg |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Revenue | 30 | 15 | 40 | 25 | 28 |
| Market share | 20 | 35 | 10 | 40 | 26 |
| Profit | 25 | 20 | 30 | 15 | 23 |
| Margin | 10 | 15 | 10 | 10 | 11 |
| CLV | 10 | 10 | 5 | 5 | 8 |
| ARPU | 5 | 5 | 5 | 5 | 5 |

This reveals massive misalignment in teams who think they agree. Force a written output: "Maximize market share, but ensure overall profit increase of at least 10%." This becomes the constraint for picking your pricing strategy.

## The BECAUSE Test — Templates

### Bad Examples
- "We priced at $99 to be competitive."
- "We priced at $99 because the spreadsheet said so."
- "We priced at $99 because the CEO said so."

### Good Examples
- "We priced at $99 BECAUSE 60% of segment B told us $100 was the threshold above which they'd reconsider, and our value advantage justifies the high end."
- "We chose subscription BECAUSE 75% of interviewed prospects preferred predictable monthly cost over per-seat licensing, and our cost-to-serve scales with usage."

### Fill-In Template
```
We priced [SKU/tier] at $[price] BECAUSE
[N]% of [segment] customers told us [specific WTP/threshold/value data point],
AND [secondary data point about competitive position or upgrade willingness].
```

## MOCA Matrix — Worked Example

Industrial pump maker vs incumbent:

```
                          HIGH IMPORTANCE
                                |
    Service network             |    Reliability (you = better)
    (incumbent better)          |    Energy efficiency (you = better)
                                |
    ─────────────────────────────────────── Performance
                                |
    Industrial design           |    Onboarding software
    (incumbent better)          |    (you = better)
                                |
                          LOW IMPORTANCE
```

### Action by Quadrant
- **Top Right (Reliability, Efficiency):** Lead all marketing here. These are your wedge.
- **Bottom Right (Onboarding software):** Try to convince customers this matters — only with proof points. If you can't prove importance, don't spend budget here.
- **Top Left (Service network):** Prepare defensive arguments. Consider bundling 3rd-party service partners or remote diagnostics.
- **Bottom Left (Industrial design):** Ignore for now.

⚠️ Performance is "as customers see it" — not as you see it. Engineers fail MOCA by self-grading.

## Value-Selling Spreadsheet (B2B)

| Input | Customer Value | Calculation |
|---|---|---|
| Current annual spend on Y | $___ | input |
| Current error rate | __% | input |
| Hours/week on manual task | __ | input |
| Average labor cost/hr | $___ | input |
| **Annual error cost** | $___ | spend × error rate |
| **Annual labor cost** | $___ | hours × 52 × rate |
| **Total addressable savings** | $___ | sum |
| **Our product cost** | $___ | your price |
| **Net annual savings** | $___ | savings − cost |
| **Payback (months)** | __ | cost ÷ monthly savings |

This reframes the conversation from "how much does it cost" to "how much do I save." Forces sales to engage with customer-specific value math. Gives procurement a quantified justification to say yes.

## Behavioral Pricing — Anchoring (The Economist Test)

### Group A
| Option | Price | Pick Rate |
|---|---|---|
| Online only | $59 | 68% |
| Print + online | $125 | 32% |

### Group B (decoy added)
| Option | Price | Pick Rate |
|---|---|---|
| Online only | $59 | 16% |
| Print only | $125 | 0% |
| **Print + online** | **$125** | **84%** |

The $125 print-only option made the $125 print+online bundle look like a no-brainer. Adding a useless decoy increased revenue ~30%.

**Rule:** Always have an anchor product. Always start B2B negotiations with a HIGH anchor.

## Behavioral Pricing — Finding Your Thresholds

### Method
Run A/B tests at multiple price points: $69, $69.99, $71, $74, $79, $89, $99
Look for **discontinuities** — cliffs where conversion drops sharply.

### Common Thresholds (Western Markets)
$40, $70, $99, $100, $199

### Rule
Stay on the cliff. $69.99 instead of $71. That $1.01 difference dropped acceptance >20% in one subscription study.

## Razor & Blades — When to Use

### Coffee Machine Example
| Option | Machine | Coffee/mo | 12-mo Total |
|---|---|---|---|
| A | $480 | $10 | $600 |
| B | $120 | $40 | $600 |

Same total. Customers strongly prefer B.

### Decision Rule
Only use razor & blades if you're 100% sure you can sell the downstream consumables:
- Lock-in mechanism (proprietary cartridges, accounts, data)
- High switching cost
- Customer cannot easily skip the consumable

If you're not sure, don't give away the razor.
